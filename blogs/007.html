Properties of Congruences

Congruences behave like equalities due to these properties:

  • Reflexive: $a \equiv a \pmod n$
  • Symmetric: If $a \equiv b \pmod n$, then $b \equiv a \pmod n$
  • Transitive: If $a \equiv b \pmod n$ and $b \equiv c \pmod n$, then $a \equiv c \pmod n$
üß†

Basic Operations with Congruences

Congruences support arithmetic operations:

  • Addition: If $a \equiv b \pmod n$ and $c \equiv d \pmod n$, then $a+c \equiv b+d \pmod n$.
  • Subtraction: If $a \equiv b \pmod n$ and $c \equiv d \pmod n$, then $a-c \equiv b-d \pmod n$.
  • Multiplication: If $a \equiv b \pmod n$ and $c \equiv d \pmod n$, then $ac \equiv bd \pmod n$.
  • Exponentiation: If $a \equiv b \pmod n$, then $a^k \equiv b^k \pmod n$ for any positive integer $k$.

Modular Inverse

The multiplicative inverse of $a$ modulo $n$ is an integer $x$ such that $ax \equiv 1 \pmod n$.

Exists if and only if $\text{gcd}(a, n) = 1$ (i.e., $a$ and $n$ are coprime).

Found using the Extended Euclidean Algorithm or trial and error for small numbers.

Fermat's Little Theorem (FLT)

If $p$ is a prime number and $a$ is not divisible by $p$:

$$ a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod p $$

Also, for any integer $a$, $a^p \equiv a \pmod p$.

Simplifies large powers in modular arithmetic!

Euler's Totient Theorem

Euler's Totient function $\phi(n)$ counts integers up to $n$ that are coprime to $n$.

Theorem: If $\text{gcd}(a, n) = 1$, then:

$$ a^{\phi(n)} \equiv 1 \pmod n $$

Generalizes Fermat's Little Theorem for non-prime $n$!

Chinese Remainder Theorem (CRT)

Solves systems of linear congruences:

$$ x \equiv a_1 \pmod{n_1} $$ $$ x \equiv a_2 \pmod{n_2} $$ $$ \dots $$

If $n_1, n_2, \dots$ are pairwise coprime, a unique solution exists modulo $N = n_1 n_2 \dots$.

Modular Algebra in Olympiads

A cornerstone of Number Theory problems!

Key Strategies:

  • Reducing Powers: Use FLT or Euler's Theorem to simplify $a^k \pmod n$.
  • Finding Inverses: Solve $ax \equiv b \pmod n$ using $x \equiv b \cdot a^{-1} \pmod n$.
  • Pattern Recognition: Look for cycles in powers modulo $n$.
  • Chinese Remainder Theorem: Solve systems of congruences.
  • Small Moduli: Test modulo 2, 3, 4, 5 for insights.

Olympiad Challenge

Find the remainder when $2^{100}$ is divided by 7

We need to find $2^{100} \pmod 7$.

Pattern in powers of 2 modulo 7:

  • $2^1 \equiv 2 \pmod 7$
  • $2^2 \equiv 4 \pmod 7$
  • $2^3 \equiv 8 \equiv 1 \pmod 7$

Since $2^3 \equiv 1 \pmod 7$:

$100 = 3 \times 33 + 1$, so $2^{100} = 2^{3 \times 33 + 1} = (2^3)^{33} \times 2^1$.

$(1)^{33} \times 2^1 \equiv 1 \times 2 \equiv 2 \pmod 7$.

Remainder: 2

B√©zout's Identity: The GCD Connection!

For any non-zero integers $a$ and $b$, there exist integers $x$ and $y$ such that $ax + by = \text{gcd}(a, b)$.

This tells us that a linear Diophantine equation $ax + by = c$ has integer solutions if and only if $\text{gcd}(a, b)$ divides $c$!

Olympiad Problem 1

Problem 1 (PRMO Level):

Find the number of positive integers $n$ such that $n^2 + 1$ is divisible by $n+1$.

Solution:

We are given that $n^2 + 1$ is divisible by $n+1$.

We can use polynomial division or manipulate the expression:

$n^2 + 1 = n^2 - 1 + 2 = (n-1)(n+1) + 2$.

Since $(n-1)(n+1)$ is clearly divisible by $n+1$, for $n^2+1$ to be divisible by $n+1$, the remainder, which is 2, must be divisible by $n+1$.

So, $n+1$ must be a divisor of 2.

The divisors of 2 are 1, 2, -1, -2.

If $n+1=1$, then $n=0$. Not a positive integer.

If $n+1=2$, then $n=1$. This is a positive integer.

If $n+1=-1$, then $n=-2$. Not a positive integer.

If $n+1=-2$, then $n=-3$. Not a positive integer.

Therefore, the only positive integer $n$ for which $n^2+1$ is divisible by $n+1$ is $n=1$.

The number of such positive integers is 1.

Olympiad Problem 2

Problem 2 (RMO Level):

Find the largest positive integer $k$ such that $2^k$ divides $20!$.

Solution:

This problem uses Legendre's Formula. Legendre's Formula states that for a prime $p$ and a positive integer $n$, the exponent of the highest power of $p$ that divides $n!$ is given by:

$$ E_p(n!) = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} \left\lfloor \frac{n}{p^i} \right\rfloor = \left\lfloor \frac{n}{p} \right\rfloor + \left\lfloor \frac{n}{p^2} \right\rfloor + \left\lfloor \frac{n}{p^3} \right\rfloor + \dots $$

In this case, $n=20$ and $p=2$.

We need to find $E_2(20!)$.

Calculate the terms:

  • $\lfloor \frac{20}{2} \rfloor = 10$
  • $\lfloor \frac{20}{4} \rfloor = 5$
  • $\lfloor \frac{20}{8} \rfloor = 2$
  • $\lfloor \frac{20}{16} \rfloor = 1$
  • $\lfloor \frac{20}{32} \rfloor = 0$ (and all subsequent terms will be 0)

Summing these values:

$k = 10 + 5 + 2 + 1 = 18$.

Therefore, the largest positive integer $k$ such that $2^k$ divides $20!$ is 18.

Story: How Number Theory Powers RSA Encryption

Alice wants to send a secret message.

She uses Bob's Public Key.

Public Key: $(n, e)$

Where $n = p \times q$ (product of two HUGE primes!)

‚Üí

Bob receives the encrypted message.

He uses his Private Key to decrypt it.

Private Key: $d$ (derived from $p, q$)

Only Bob knows $p$ and $q$!

The security of RSA encryption relies on the fact that it's computationally very hard to factor a large number ($n$) back into its two prime factors ($p$ and $q$).

This means finding those "secret" prime building blocks is super tough for hackers!
